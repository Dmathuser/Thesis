\begin{abstract}
Intrinsic motivation methods for exploration in reinforcement learning
are vulnerable to the Noisy-TV problem, where stochastic stimuli trap
agents into endlessly seeking novelty from non-informative distractors.
We propose a \emph{predict-then-verify} principle: instead of
rewarding predicted information gain, reward only the measured
decrease in model uncertainty after each transition.  We instantiate
this principle in two ways: Temporal PIG (TPIG), which uses
KL-divergence, and a learning progress (LP) baseline using MSE.
Both are minimal modifications to the Predicted Information Gain
(PIG) algorithm.  Evaluated on grid-world environments with up to
100 states, both TPIG and LP dramatically outperform PIG on
distraction rate, exploration speed, and model accuracy, confirming
that closed-loop verification of learning is the key to resisting
stochastic distractors regardless of divergence measure.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Reinforcement learning (RL) agents learn by taking actions in an
environment and receiving rewards that guide future
behavior~\citep{Sutton1998,Kaelbling1996RLsurvey}.  When environmental
rewards are sparse or absent, agents must generate their own
\emph{intrinsic} rewards to drive
exploration~\citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1908-06976,FToCFaIM:Jurgen,Oudeyer2007intrinsic}.
Common intrinsic reward methods include count-based
novelty~\citep{DBLP:journals/corr/TangHFSCDSTA16,Bellemare2016counting},
prediction-error
curiosity~\citep{Pathak2017ICM,Rein:VIME}, and information-theoretic
approaches~\citep{10.3389/fncir.2013.00037}.

A fundamental weakness of many intrinsic motivation methods is the
\emph{Noisy-TV problem}~\citep{Burda2019curiosity}.
Consider an environment containing a television with a remote control.
Each button press displays a random, never-before-seen channel.
Novelty-seeking agents become trapped, perpetually pressing the remote
because each press yields a ``new'' state, even though no useful
information about the environment is being learned.

Predicted Information Gain
(PIG)~\citep{10.3389/fncir.2013.00037} selects actions by estimating,
via KL-divergence, how much each action would improve the agent's
transition model.  Although PIG is more principled than raw
novelty-seeking, it still falls prey to the Noisy-TV: the stochastic
transitions from button presses yield high predicted divergence, luring
the agent back to the distractor.

The core issue is that PIG \emph{predicts} information gain but never
\emph{confirms} that learning actually occurred.  We propose a
\textbf{predict-then-verify} approach: compute the predicted
information gain both before and after incorporating a new transition,
and use the \emph{actual decrease} as a TD reward.  We instantiate
this principle as Temporal PIG (TPIG, using KL-divergence) and a
learning progress baseline (LP, using MSE), and show that both resist
the Noisy TV across environments ranging from 16 to 100 states.
Many curiosity mechanisms are fundamentally open-loop: information
gain, prediction error, and novelty signals can all remain high in
stochastic environments even when no meaningful knowledge is acquired.
We show that closing this loop by conditioning rewards on measured
model improvement works under different divergence measures,
indicating that the verification mechanism, not the specific curiosity
signal, is the critical ingredient.

\section{Related work}

\subsection{Intrinsic motivation for exploration}

Intrinsic motivation methods let agents generate their own learning
signals~\citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-1908-06976,FToCFaIM:Jurgen},
realized in several families of algorithms.

\emph{Count-based} methods reward visiting novel states by tracking
visitation counts.  \citet{Bellemare2016counting} introduced
pseudo-counts to unify count-based exploration with intrinsic
motivation in high-dimensional spaces, and
\citet{DBLP:journals/corr/TangHFSCDSTA16} extended this approach
using hash functions.  However, count-based methods are directly
vulnerable to the Noisy-TV problem: every channel change registers
as a novel state, producing unbounded reward from a single
distractor.

\emph{Prediction-error} methods use the agent's inability to predict
outcomes as a reward signal.  \citet{Pathak2017ICM} proposed the
Intrinsic Curiosity Module (ICM), which trains a forward dynamics
model and uses its prediction error as intrinsic reward.
\citet{Rein:VIME} proposed VIME, which maximizes information gain
about environment dynamics using Bayesian neural networks.
\citet{Burda2019RND} introduced Random Network Distillation (RND),
which measures novelty as the prediction error of a randomly
initialized fixed network.  While more robust than count-based
methods, \citet{Burda2019curiosity} demonstrated that prediction-error
agents can still be attracted to inherently unpredictable transitions
such as those produced by a Noisy TV.

\emph{Information-theoretic} methods, including PIG~\citep{10.3389/fncir.2013.00037},
estimate the expected change in the agent's world model using
information-theoretic quantities such as KL-divergence.
\citet{10.3389/fncir.2013.00037} showed that PIG-based exploration
learns environment structure faster than reward-free baselines.
\citet{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2007-13442} provided theoretical
analysis of sample-efficient exploration strategies.

\subsection{The noisy-TV problem}

The Noisy-TV problem was identified by \citet{Burda2019curiosity} and
remains an open challenge; \citet{Ladosz_2022} note that most
intrinsic motivation methods remain
susceptible~\citep{DBLP:journals/corr/abs-2008-04388}.

TPIG can also be viewed as an instantiation of \emph{learning
progress}~\citep{Oudeyer2007intrinsic}, which rewards reductions in
prediction error over time.  We make this connection explicit by
also evaluating an MSE-based learning progress baseline (LP), allowing
a direct comparison of divergence measures within the same TD
framework.  Both TPIG and LP operate directly on the agent's
transition model without auxiliary networks, making them minimal
modifications to PIG.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Temporal difference learning}

In temporal difference (TD) learning~\citep{Sutton1998}, the agent
maintains Q-values $Q(s,a)$ for each state-action
pair~\citep{Watkins1992Qlearning} and updates them after each
transition $(s,a,s')$ using:
\begin{equation}
  Q(s,a) \leftarrow Q(s,a) + \alpha \left[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)\right],
  \label{eq:TDQVF}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the reward, $\alpha$ is the learning rate, and $\gamma$
is the discount factor~\citep{Sutton1998}.

\subsection{Predicted information gain (PIG)}

PIG uses the KL-divergence~\citep{Kullback1951information} to estimate
how much an agent's transition model would change if it took a
particular action.  Let
$\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}$ denote the agent's estimated transition
probabilities from state $s$ under action $a$, and let
$\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}^{s \to s^*}$ denote the updated estimate
after hypothetically observing a transition to $s^*$.  The predicted
information gain is:
\begin{equation}
  \textrm{PIG}(s,a) := \sum_{s^*} \hat{\Theta}_{sas^*}\,
  D_{\textrm{KL}}\!\left(\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}^{s \to s^*}
  \;\|\;\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}\right),
  \label{eq:PIG}
\end{equation}
where $D_{\textrm{KL}}$ is the KL-divergence.  PIG selects the action
$a$ maximizing this quantity.  As data accumulates,
$\hat{\Theta} \to \Theta$:
\[
  \lim_{n \to \infty} D_{\textrm{KL}}(\Theta_{sa\cdot} \| \hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}) =
  \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{s'} \Theta_{sas'} \log_2 \frac{\Theta_{sas'}}{\hat{\Theta}_{sas'}} = 0,
\]
since each ratio $\Theta_{sas'}/\hat{\Theta}_{sas'} \to 1$ and
$\log_2(1) = 0$.

\subsection{The noisy-TV problem}

When the environment contains a Noisy TV, the stochastic channel
changes produce transitions to many distinct states, each with high
predicted information gain.  PIG's action selection thus repeatedly
favors the TV because the weighted average over all possible channel
outcomes remains large, even though the transitions carry no useful
structural information about the environment.

\section{The predict-then-verify principle}

TPIG modifies PIG by measuring the \emph{actual} information gained
after each transition rather than relying on predictions alone.  After
observing transition $(s,a,s')$, TPIG computes the predicted
information gain both before and after updating the internal model:
\begin{equation}
  r_{\textrm{TPIG}}(s,a,s') :=
  D_{\textrm{KL}}\!\bigl(\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}^{s \to s',\,t-1}
  \;\|\;\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}^{t-1}\bigr)
  - D_{\textrm{KL}}\!\bigl(\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}^{s \to s',\,t}
  \;\|\;\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}^{t}\bigr),
  \label{eq:TPIGReward}
\end{equation}
where superscripts $t{-}1$ and $t$ denote the model before and after
incorporating the observed transition.  If genuine learning occurred,
the remaining information decreases and the reward is positive.
Both KL-divergence evaluations reuse PIG's existing computation, so
TPIG adds only one additional divergence calculation per step.
This reward updates Q-values via standard TD
learning~\eqref{eq:TDQVF}, with $\alpha = 0.9$ and $\gamma = 0.9$.
Q-values are initialized to 1, providing an optimistic prior that
encourages exploration of all actions before learned rewards dominate.
TPIG selects the action with the highest Q-value:
\[
  \textrm{TPIG}(s) = \argmax_a Q(s,a).
\]

\subsection{The closed-minded model problem}

After a single observation of $(s,a,s')$, PIG records that transition
as 100\% probable.  On subsequent visits, the predicted and observed
distributions are identical, the KL-divergence returns~0, and PIG
considers the action fully learned, even if the true transition is
stochastic.  This ``closed-minded'' behavior causes PIG to neglect
areas visited only once.

Both PIG and TPIG address this by initializing the total observed
transition count to~1 rather than~0, equivalent to a uniform Dirichlet
prior.  This ensures the KL-divergence always indicates remaining
information.  As observations accumulate, the prior is overwhelmed
and the model converges.

\subsection{Handling infinities}

When $\hat{\Theta}_{sa\cdot}$ is empty (no transitions observed),
the KL-divergence returns infinity.  These values are replaced with 0
in the reward calculation.  Negative rewards (which can occur when
discovering a new state temporarily increases uncertainty) are clamped
to 0 to avoid penalizing exploration.  Similarly, any Q-values that
are infinite are normalized to~0 before the TD update is applied.

\subsection{Why TPIG resists the noisy TV}

PIG predicts high information gain from the TV because the model
would change substantially.  However, TPIG recomputes the predicted
gain after the update and finds it is still high: each observation
barely reduces the remaining uncertainty because the TV states are so
numerous.  The reward is near zero, and TPIG's Q-values for
TV-related actions decay.  More precisely, for a stochastic transition
with $k$ equally likely outcomes, the $n$-th observation reduces
predicted information gain by only $O(1/n)$, so the reward vanishes.
The same argument applies to LP's MSE reward.  Structured transitions
produce larger, persistent reductions under either measure.

In summary, PIG takes the action it \emph{predicts} will teach it the
most.  Both TPIG and LP take the action \emph{experienced} to yield
the most actual learning, a crucial distinction that makes the
predict-then-verify approach robust to stochastic distractors.

\subsection{Epsilon-greedy variants}

We also evaluate $\epsilon$-greedy versions of both algorithms:
EPIG and ETPIG.  With probability $\epsilon$ (set to 10\% unless
otherwise noted), the agent selects a random action instead of the
greedy choice.

\section{Experimental setup}

\subsection{Grid-world simulations}

We test on four grid-world environments of increasing size
(Figure~\ref{Fig:Sim}):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{4-TV} ($4 \times 4$): Multiple Noisy TVs.
  \item \textbf{1-TV} ($5 \times 5$): A single Noisy TV.
  \item \textbf{No-TV} ($6 \times 6$): No distractors (control).
  \item \textbf{10x10-2TV} ($10 \times 10$): Two Noisy TVs with
    internal walls creating corridors and rooms (100 states).
\end{itemize}

In each environment, actions move the agent in one of four cardinal
directions.  Movement succeeds with 90\% probability; with 10\%
probability the agent remains in place.  Walking into a wall always
leaves the agent stationary.  Activating a Noisy TV increments an unbounded noise variable in the
state, so each activation produces a unique state, simulating a TV
with an inexhaustible supply of channels.  Importantly, the noise
variable does not affect the actual transition dynamics: all TV
states share the same transition probabilities regardless of the
current channel.  The noise is therefore a pure distractor: it
inflates the apparent state space without adding any learnable
structure.

All agents start in position~5.  Each episode runs for 1000 time
steps (3000 for the 10x10-2TV environment) with no goal state.  Every policy is run for 100 independent
trials per simulation.  The source code and simulation data are publicly
available.\footnote{Repository URL withheld for double-blind review.}

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Distraction rate}: Cumulative Noisy-TV activations.
  \item \textbf{Learning rate}: Number of undiscovered state
    transitions remaining.
  \item \textbf{Model accuracy}: KL-divergence between the agent's
    model and the true transition probabilities (excluding
    undiscovered transitions, which would contribute infinity).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Policies compared}

Six policies are evaluated: Random Walk (uniform random baseline),
PIG, EPIG ($\epsilon$-greedy PIG), TPIG, ETPIG ($\epsilon$-greedy
TPIG), and LP (Learning Progress, a baseline that uses the decrease
in prediction MSE rather than KL-divergence as its TD reward, using
the same Q-learning framework as TPIG).

\section{Results}

\subsection{Distraction rate}

Figures~\ref{Fig:DR4TV} and~\ref{Fig:DR1TV} show cumulative
distractions.  PIG is heavily distracted, with its count growing
steadily.  Random Walk is distracted least since it has no bias toward

\subsection{Effect of epsilon}

We tested $\epsilon$ in increments of 20\%.  Higher $\epsilon$ reduces
distractions in the 4-TV simulation, but in 1-TV and No-TV, lower
$\epsilon$ yields better long-term ETPIG performance, since TPIG's
learned avoidance makes heavy randomization counterproductive.  In the
1-TV ETPIG results (not shown), we observed a pivot around step~100
where all $\epsilon$ values shift from exploration to directed TV
avoidance.  Pure TPIG ($\epsilon = 0$) exhibits early artifacts
smoothed by even small $\epsilon$ values.

\section{Discussion}

By conditioning rewards on the measured decrease in model uncertainty,
the predict-then-verify approach transforms exploration into a
closed-loop feedback system.  The Noisy TV generates large predictions
but negligible actual learning, so Q-values for TV-related actions
naturally decay under both TPIG and LP.  Because the reward converges
to zero as the model approaches the true distribution, both methods
inherit TD convergence guarantees~\citep{Sutton1998}, unlike open-loop
curiosity methods that can be permanently misled by stochastic stimuli.

\subsection{Learning dynamics}

Both TPIG and LP exhibit two-phase behavior: initial broad exploration
while accumulating data, followed by directed exploration once Q-values
stabilize.  PIG may outperform them during the first phase, but both
surpass PIG on all metrics in the second.  This pattern is consistent
across all 100 trials, as the narrow error bands confirm.

The duration of this initial learning phase scales with environment
size.  In the $4 \times 4$ 4-TV environment, the crossover occurs
around time step~200; in the $5 \times 5$ 1-TV environment, around
time step~300; in the $6 \times 6$ No-TV environment, around step~400;
and in the $10 \times 10$ 10x10-2TV environment, around step~600.  This approximately linear scaling suggests that TPIG's
startup cost is predictable and proportional to the number of
state-action pairs, representing 20--40\% of the episode length across the environments
tested.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} shows a sensitivity analysis of $\alpha$ and
$\gamma$ on the 1-TV environment for both TPIG and LP.  For TPIG,
high $\alpha$ with low $\gamma$ causes instability (521 distractions
at $\alpha{=}0.9$, $\gamma{=}0.1$), but all configurations with
$\gamma \geq 0.5$ outperform PIG.  LP is far more robust: it
outperforms PIG in \emph{every} parameter combination, including the
setting that is catastrophic for TPIG.  This confirms that the
predict-then-verify mechanism is robust to hyperparameter choice, and
that MSE is inherently more stable than KL-divergence for this
application.

\subsection{The role of epsilon}

In environments with many distractors, higher $\epsilon$ helps both
EPIG and ETPIG escape loops.  In cleaner environments, lower
$\epsilon$ yields better ETPIG performance because TPIG already
learns to avoid distractors; additional randomization only slows
convergence.  For PIG-based methods, higher $\epsilon$ is consistently
helpful since PIG lacks any mechanism to learn from its mistakes.
LP was tested only in greedy mode; its strong distraction avoidance
(Table~\ref{tab:summary}) suggests it may benefit less from
$\epsilon$-greedy randomization than PIG does.

\subsection{Comparison with other approaches}

Unlike RND~\citep{Burda2019RND} or ICM~\citep{Pathak2017ICM},
neither TPIG nor LP requires auxiliary networks, and both operate
without any prior knowledge of which states are distractors or how
the environment is structured.  The LP baseline
validates the generality of the predict-then-verify principle:
replacing KL-divergence with MSE preserves noise resistance and yields
even lower distraction rates, while both achieve comparable
exploration speed and model accuracy.  The choice of divergence measure is secondary to the closed-loop
verification mechanism, and practitioners may prefer MSE for its
simplicity and stronger distraction avoidance.

\subsection{Limitations}

The current study has several limitations.  First, although we test
on environments up to 100 states, they remain tabular.  Scaling to
continuous state spaces would require function approximation for the
transition model and Q-values.  Second, our unbounded noise model (infinitely many unique TV states)
represents a worst case for novelty-based methods; real-world
distractors with finite state spaces would be easier to overcome.  Third, we do not combine TPIG's intrinsic reward with
extrinsic rewards for goal-directed tasks.  The $\pm 1\sigma$ bands in all
figures confirm that the reported results are statistically robust.

\section{Conclusion and future work}

We introduced the predict-then-verify principle for curiosity-driven
exploration and demonstrated it through two instantiations: TPIG
(KL-divergence) and LP (MSE).  Both dramatically outperform PIG across four grid-world environments
ranging from 16 to 100 states and from zero to four distractors,
with LP achieving the strongest distraction resistance and TPIG the
best model accuracy.  The convergence of results across divergence measures confirms that
the key mechanism is closed-loop verification of learning, not the
specific metric used; the choice between KL-divergence and MSE
becomes a tunable design parameter rather than a fundamental
algorithmic decision.

Future directions include scaling to continuous environments with
function approximation, combining intrinsic and extrinsic rewards,
and investigating whether a weighted combination of KL-divergence and
MSE rewards can achieve both LP's distraction resistance and TPIG's
model accuracy.
The principle's simplicity and representation-independence make it a
promising candidate for integration into deep RL curiosity
methods~\citep{Pathak2017ICM,Burda2019RND}.
